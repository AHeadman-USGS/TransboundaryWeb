Unuk Watershed

The Unuk River drains 2476 square kilometers, from the Boundary Ranges in Canada to the Behm Canal, Alaska. Mountain ridges separate the watershed from the Iskut River - a tributary of the Stikine - to the north, and the Salmon River to the south.

Background

The Tahltan and Tligngit Sanyaa Kwaan recognize the Unuk watershed as part of their traditional territories, and they remain stewards of the land. Misty Fjords National Monument Wilderness, administered as part of Tongass National Forest, encompasses the Alaskan portion of the watershed. In Canada, Lava Forks and Border Lake Provincial Parks protect areas immediately adjacent to the border.



Mineral deposits in the upper region of the watershed are associated with the Stikinia geologic terrane. In addition to extensive placer mining for gold, hard-rock mining has focused on large porphyry gold, silver, copper, and molybdenum deposits. Within the Unuk watershed, there are four large-scale mines that are currently operating or in the later stages of permitting and development.

Research and Data

The USGS operates a streamgage near the mouth of the Unuk River. Data including stream flow conditions, continuous water quality monitoring, and discrete water quality sampling are available through the National Water Information System (NWIS).

USGS field crews travel to the Unuk a few times each year to collect additional samples. Data is available at NWIS.
